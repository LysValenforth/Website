The Tree Who Loved the Sun

Apr 25, 2025

Once, there was a small tree growing all alone in the middle of a quiet field. He was young, with soft green leaves and a slender trunk. When the wind passed by, he swayed almost like he was dancing. And when the rain came, he laughed, his branches tickled by the drops.

But more than anything, the little tree longed for the sun.

Every morning, the sun would rise over the horizon, warming the earth, touching everything with her golden light. The little tree would look up at her; she was beautiful. She was brilliant. And he wanted nothing more than to be near her.

“I want to touch her,” the tree whispered to the wind. “She’s so far away, but if I grow tall enough, I know I can reach her.”

And so, the tree grew.

Day after day, he stretched his trunk higher, his branches reaching, always reaching for the sky. His roots dug deeper into the earth, holding him steady as he reached for the sun.

The birds noticed. They flew down and perched on his branches. “Why are you growing so fast, little tree?” a sparrow asked.

“I want to be with the sun,” he said. “She’s so far away. But if I grow tall enough, maybe I can reach her.”

The sparrow tilted her head, a little confused, then fluttered her wings. “You can’t touch the sun, little tree. She belongs to the sky.”

But the tree only looked up, dreaming of the day he could finally touch her.

Seasons passed, and the tree grew taller, reaching higher and higher, above the fields, over the hills. His branches stretched wide, but still, the sun remained out of reach.

One day, a fox came to rest beneath his shade. “You’re getting too tall, little tree,” the fox said. “You’re growing so fast, but you’re wearing yourself out. Your bark is cracking, your leaves are dry. Why do you strain yourself like this?”

“I love the sun,” the tree answered, his voice shaky, filled with desperation. “I want to be closer to her. So I have to grow.”

The fox looked up at the sun, then back at the tree. “Be careful,” the fox warned. “The higher you grow, the farther your roots will pull from the earth. You can’t be with the sun. And in reaching for her, you might lose everything.”

But the tree didn’t listen. He just kept growing.

He grew higher and higher, until the birds stopped coming and the squirrels no longer climbed. Even the clouds seemed to draw closer now. He had become too tall, too lonely, but the sun was still so far away. No matter how high he reached, she stayed in her sky.

And now, the heat began to hurt. His leaves curled at the edges. His bark peeled away like old paper. The wind no longer played with him—it pushed against him, as if to slow him down.

He was tired. So tired. But still, he loved the sun.

Then one summer day, his tallest branch—the one that had reached the highest—snapped. It fell to the ground with a soft thud, and the tree trembled. He had stretched so far, reached so high, dreaming of the day he could touch the sun.

But the crack was only the beginning. Soon, another branch broke—this one louder, sharper, echoing across the field. The branch struck the earth, splitting the ground beneath it.

The tree swayed, unbalanced now, his bark splitting, roots pulling free from the earth. And with one final, heart-shattering crash, the tree fell.

He slammed into the ground with a deafening thud, his trunk snapping, his branches flung wide, splintering like shattered dreams. The destruction echoed through the field, all for the sun. All for a light he was never meant to hold.

He had reached too far, but in the end, he had fallen too far.

Now, he was nothing more than a broken branch, still and lifeless, lost to the soil he had once loved, the very ground he thought he would outgrow.

And the sun? She kept shining above, brilliant, untouched, unmoved. She hadn’t even noticed his fall.

And the little tree, lying in pieces, roots exposed, dreams scattered like leaves in the wind, murmured weakly, “I thought I could reach the sun, but now... I see I’ve reached the end.”
